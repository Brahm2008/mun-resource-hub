Learn MUN Basics

A beginner-friendly introduction based on procedures and patterns commonly seen in Indian school and student MUN conferences.

What is MUN?

Model United Nations (MUN) is an academic simulation where students represent countries, debate international issues and work towards diplomatic solutions. In Indian school and student MUN circuits, the exact style of debate may differ by conference, but the core skills remain the same: research, debate, negotiation and teamwork.

A Quick Note on Procedure

There is no single universal MUN procedure used everywhere. Many Indian conferences follow adapted or hybrid Rules of Procedure, often influenced by THIMUN, HMUN, UNA-USA or custom school-specific formats. This page focuses on commonly seen patterns in Indian MUNs, but delegates should always read the official Rules of Procedure provided by their conference.

Common Terms in MUNs

Delegate

The student representing a country, portfolio or assigned role in committee.

Executive Board (EB)

The chairs or dais members who moderate the committee and enforce procedure.

GSL

The General Speakers List, used in many MUNs for formal speaking order. This is usually an inexhaustive list i.e. any number of speakers can participate in it, per delegate speeches are usually timed around 90 seconds.

Moderated Caucus

A timed, structured discussion on a specific sub-topic with shorter speaking slots. These are exhaustive i.e. only a certain, specified number of speakers can be addressed in this.

Unmoderated Caucus

An informal session where delegates move around, discuss and build blocs.

Working Paper / Draft Resolution

The written document where delegates combine ideas, clauses and proposed solutions. It is basically the summary of all the debates and discussions done in the conference so far.

Typical Flow in Indian MUN Conferences

  1. Roll Call (often with “Present” or “Present and Voting”)
  2. Agenda introduction or topic briefing (if applicable)
  3. General Speakers List or opening speeches.
  4. Moderated caucuses on sub-topics
  5. Unmoderated caucuses for bloc formation and drafting
  6. Working papers or draft resolutions
  7. Final debate, clause discussion and voting procedure

The exact order and terminology may differ depending on the conference handbook.

Common Points & Motions in Indian MUNs

Frequently Seen Points

  • Point of Personal Privilege: Raised to address urgent and personal comfort isuues such as adjusting the AC temperature, adjusting the mic etc. Note that bio break and water break does not require the use of this point.
  • Point of Parliamentary Inquiry: Raised to address any procedural doubt regarding the flow of committee.
  • Point of Order (only in some conferences): Very uncommon but it is usually used to point out factual errors in other delegate's speech. In some conferences, however it is used to point out inaccuracy in committee proceedings.
  • Point of Information: To ask questions from other delegates based on their speech. Its use varies according to the descretion of the chair.

Frequently Seen Motions

  • Motion to open debate
  • Motion to enter or continue the GSL
  • Motion for a moderated caucus. The commonly used verbatim to raise a motion for moderated caucus is: " The delegate of ______ would like to suspend the formal debate and enter into a moderated caucus on topic ______ for total time being _____ and per speakers time being ______."
  • Motion for an unmoderated caucus. The commonly used verbatim to raise a motion for moderated caucus is: " The delegate of ______ would like to suspend the formal debate and enter into an unmoderated caucus for total time being _____."
  • Motion to introduce a working paper or draft resolution
  • Motion to close debate / move into voting procedure

Beginner Tips

  • Read the conference’s Rules of Procedure before anything else. These are usually given in Background Guides.
  • Do not assume every MUN follows the same format just because your last one did.
  • Always try preparing some moderated caucus topics according to agenda before hand.
  • Focus more on substance and diplomacy than on dramatic language.
  • Prepare flexible points and solutions, not just one memorised speech. Note that paper reading is allowed in MUNs.
