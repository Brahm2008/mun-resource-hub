Research & Resources

A practical starting point for MUN preparation: research, position papers, resolution writing and pre-conference planning.

How to Research for MUN

What to Research First

  • Your country’s official stance on the agenda and foreign policy position
  • Basic background of the agenda and why it matters
  • Major stakeholders and countries involved
  • Recent developments, treaties and major incidents
  • Past UN actions, resolutions and committee reports

Useful Sources to Start With

  • UN official websites and committee pages
  • Government ministry or embassy statements
  • Reliable news platforms for current developments like Reuters, AlJazeera etc.
  • Think tanks or policy research organisations
  • Conference background guides and handbooks
  • Note that Wikipedia, local news channels are not valid sources for factual information. Always refer to background guide/committee handbook for acceptable sources of information.

What to Include in a Position Paper

1. Agenda Overview

Briefly explain the issue in context. Show that you understand the topic beyond just definitions.

2. Country Position

Explain your assigned country’s stance clearly. Mention priorities, concerns and policy interests.

3. Past Actions

Include major treaties, voting patterns, speeches or previous involvement related to the agenda.

4. Proposed Solutions

Suggest realistic and diplomatic solutions that align with your country’s interests and committee scope.

Resolution Writing Basics

A resolution is a formal document that proposes solutions. In many student MUNs, especially school conferences, it is useful to understand the difference between the two main parts:

Preambulatory Clauses

  • Provide context and background
  • Refer to past resolutions, treaties or concerns
  • Usually begin with phrases like “Recognising”, “Recalling”, or “Concerned about”. Refer to UN site for all the preambulatory clauses.

Operative Clauses

  • Present actual solutions and actions
  • Should be clear, practical and relevant to the committee
  • Usually begin with phrases like “Encourages”, “Requests”, or “Calls upon”. Refer to UN site for all the operative clauses.

Practical MUN Prep Checklist

  • Read the background guide carefully and highlight key problem areas.
  • Prepare a one-page country profile for quick revision.
  • Write 5–7 strong points for your opening speech. It is always advisable to prepare your GSL/Opening Speech before hand.
  • Make a list of likely allies and opposing blocs.
  • Prepare 3–5 realistic solutions that fit your country’s position.
  • Understand the conference-specific Rules of Procedure before committee begins.
  • Keep notes for moderated caucuses, unmoderated caucuses and drafting.

Beginner Tips That Actually Help

Don’t Memorise Everything

Focus on understanding your country’s position and 3–4 strong arguments instead of trying to memorise full scripts. Relevance to agenda is more important than length of speeches.

Listen for Points of Information

If you are nervous about rebutting, listen carefully and then raise a point you can build on. Points of Information are good areas to build your scores.

Clear, Concise and Coherent

These are the three points which should always be keept in mind while drafting speeches.Make your speeches clear and short so that you dont rush up. Always stick to the Agenda, avoid repetetion and unnecessary backgrounds.

Know the Handbook

Many mistakes happen because delegates prepare the topic but ignore the conference’s specific Rules of Procedure and Code of Conduct. So go through it beforehand.

Participate Not Win

Participating mindset is more helpful in MUNs than Winning mindset. So always try your best to participate in the debate rather than not speaking on the pretext of being relevant.
